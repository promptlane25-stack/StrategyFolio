Objection Handling · Garage Prospects

When they say
"it's too expensive"
here's what you say.

Seven objections you'll hear on every garage call. Read this before the conversation, not during it — you should know the answers cold.

► THE ONE PRINCIPLE BEHIND EVERY RESPONSE

Never defend your price. Always reframe the comparison. They're comparing you to software — your job is to move the conversation to what the software doesn't do.

01
The headline price objection
Most common · 80% of calls
THEY SAY
My Garage CRM is only £139 a month. Why would I pay you £549?
THE TRAP

They're comparing a tool to a service. Don't argue with the number — reframe what they're buying. The moment you start justifying £549 against £139 on features, you lose.

WHAT YOU SAY
"That's a fair question, and honestly — My Garage CRM is great software. If you don't have any system for running your workshop, get it. It's a hundred and forty quid well spent. But here's the thing — that tool sends reminders to customers you already have. It doesn't bring new ones in. It doesn't show up on Google when someone types 'MOT near me'. It doesn't write your blog, post to your Facebook, or keep your website alive. That's a completely different job — and that's the one I do. You need both. They're not the same thing."
THEN PIVOT TO

Ask immediately: "Out of curiosity — how many new customers walked through your door last month that found you online?" Their answer (usually "I don't know" or "not many") hands you the next part of the conversation.

Never: list features against theirs. Never apologise for the price. Never offer a discount in the first breath.
02
The "I already have software" objection
2nd most common
THEY SAY
I already use [MOT Manager / Motasoft / etc.] for all that.
THE TRAP

They're saying this to end the conversation. Don't attack their software — agree with it and reposition yourself as complementary, not a replacement.

WHAT YOU SAY
"Perfect — keep it. Seriously. That's the engine room of your business and I'd never ask you to rip it out. But your garage software is the cash register. I'm the shop window. MOT Manager will never build you a website, it won't rank you on Google, and it won't write a single word of content. What I do sits on top of what you've already got and feeds it more customers. You run them through MOT Manager once they walk through the door — that's its job, not mine."
THEN PIVOT TO

"Can I ask — when was the last time someone found your garage through a Google search and booked without phoning first?" Works every time. Almost nobody can answer.

Key: Position yourself as "works with" not "instead of". This lowers their defences instantly.
03
The DIY objection
Usually from younger owners
THEY SAY
I can just do this stuff myself — I've got a Facebook page already.
THE TRAP

Don't tell them they're wrong. Agree that they can — then make the time cost obvious.

WHAT YOU SAY
"Absolutely, you can. The question is whether you will, every week, for the next two years. Every garage owner I've met has started a Facebook page and a website. Most of them — and I mean most — haven't posted to either in six months because they've been busy fixing cars. That's not a criticism, that's just the job. What I do is make sure this stuff actually gets done. Every week. Whether you've got a busy day or not. At your shop rate of — what, forty pounds an hour? — six hours a month of this admin is two hundred and forty quid of your time gone. You might as well be paying me and spending those hours on a ramp making real money."
THEN PIVOT TO

"When was the last time you posted something on your Facebook page?" If it's more than a month ago (it always is), you've just proved the point without saying a word.

The maths is your friend here: their own hourly rate beats yours.
04
The "prove it" objection
Legitimate · handle carefully
THEY SAY
How do I know this will actually work? Where are your case studies?
THE TRAP

Don't oversell. Don't invent numbers. You're new — own it and turn it into a pricing advantage. This is the honesty play, and it closes more deals than a fake case study ever would.

WHAT YOU SAY
"Fair. I'm not going to pretend I've got fifty garages on the books — I'm building this consultancy and you'd be one of my first clients. Here's what that actually means for you: I'm hungry. I'll give your garage a level of attention a bigger agency never would. I'll answer the phone when you call. And because you're early, I can do something I won't be able to do in a year — I can knock the setup fee in half if you're willing to be my case study. One condition: after six months, if it's working, you let me put your name and numbers on my website. That's the deal."
THEN PIVOT TO

"Does that sound fair?" Use silence afterwards. Don't keep talking. They'll either accept the framing or raise the real objection.

Use sparingly: only drop the 50% setup discount for your first 2–3 clients. Martlesham is one of them.
05
The "contract" objection
Risk-averse owners
THEY SAY
I'm not signing up to £549 a month for 12 months on someone I've just met.
THE TRAP

Don't try to lock them in. A month-to-month deal removes the objection and forces you to perform — which you'd do anyway.

WHAT YOU SAY
"Then don't. I don't do annual contracts — it's month-to-month from day one. Thirty days' notice, cancel any time. If I'm not earning my fee by month three you'll know about it and you should cancel. The only thing I'd ask is give it a proper ninety days — SEO and content don't work overnight, and it'd be unfair on both of us to judge it after two weeks. But I'm not going to tie you into anything. Your money, your choice, every single month."
THEN PIVOT TO

Push straight to next step: "If there's no lock-in, what's actually stopping us starting next week?"

This is your strongest close for Martlesham-sized garages. Risk removal beats discount every time.
06
The "AI is a scam" objection
Older owners mainly
THEY SAY
All this AI stuff is a load of rubbish. I don't trust it.
THE TRAP

Don't defend AI. Don't lecture them. Reframe the word entirely — they're reacting to the label, not the thing.

WHAT YOU SAY
"I hear that a lot, and honestly, a lot of the AI hype is rubbish. Forget the buzzword for a second. What I actually do is pretty simple — I build you a proper website, I make sure Google knows you exist, I write the blog posts and the social posts so you don't have to, and I set up automatic reminders so customers come back. AI is just a tool I use to get it done faster and cheaper than an agency would. You don't need to trust AI — you need to trust the results. Let me show you what the website would look like before you commit to a penny."
THEN PIVOT TO

Offer a free website mockup. "Give me your garage name and I'll build you a sample page for free. No obligation. If you like it, we talk. If not, you've lost nothing."

Remember: strip the word "AI" out of your sales language entirely with skeptical owners. Sell the outcome.
07
The stall
Soft "no" dressed as a "maybe"
THEY SAY
Let me think about it and get back to you.
THE TRAP

This is not an objection — it's a polite brush-off. If you let them leave without a specific next step, you will never hear from them again. Ruthless follow-through starts here.

WHAT YOU SAY
"Absolutely, take the time you need. Can I ask one thing though — what specifically do you want to think about? Because if it's the price, I can tell you right now what the cheapest entry point looks like. If it's whether it'll work, I can show you three examples in ten minutes. If it's timing, we can start any month you want. Nine times out of ten 'let me think about it' means I haven't explained something properly — so what's the actual question?"
THEN PIVOT TO

If they still want to go: "No problem. Let me put fifteen minutes in the diary for Thursday afternoon — I'll call you back then and you can tell me yes, no, or what you need from me to decide. Does Thursday at 3 work?" Lock in a specific time, not "next week".

Ruthless rule: no follow-up call scheduled = no deal. Don't leave without a time in the diary.

The ideal call flow

1

Open with their pain, not your pitch. "How are you finding new customers at the moment?" Let them describe the gap themselves.

2

Listen for 3 minutes before selling anything. They will tell you which objections are coming. Adjust.

3

Anchor high, land mid. Mention Complete first (£899), then position Growth (£549) as "what most garages your size go for". Starter is the safety net.

4

Handle 1 objection max before asking for the next step. Don't let the call become a debate. Reframe, pivot, close.

5

Always leave with a diary slot. Next call, next mockup, next visit. A garage owner who says "I'll think about it" with no date is already gone.

  1. Never say "only"Never say "it's only £549". The word "only" is a tell. Say "it's £549" with a straight face and wait.

  2. Silence is a weaponAfter you've said the price or handled the objection, shut up. The first person to speak loses. Count to ten in your head.

  3. Sell the outcome, not the toolNobody buys "a website with Calendly integration". They buy "fifteen more MOT bookings a month without answering the phone".

  4. Never trash the competitorAlways say their software is great. It makes you look confident and stops them defending it instead of listening to you.

  5. Book the next step on the callAlways leave with a specific date and time. Never "I'll email you next week". That's a lost deal.

  6. Walk away if you have toIf they're never going to buy, end the call politely and move on. Your time is worth more than chasing a no.
